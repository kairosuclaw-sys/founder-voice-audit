Voice-faithful rewrite

We keep hiring senior engineers who are technically excellent but cannot explain what they are building to anyone outside their team.

So the founder becomes the translator. Board meetings, customer calls, investor updates — same person converting engineering output into language the rest of the business can act on.

That is not a leadership problem. It is a communication architecture problem.

The fix is not "teach engineers to present better." It is hiring people who can hold both contexts without needing a founder in the middle every time.

Raw founder note

Everyone tells you to raise your prices. That advice is correct and also useless. The reason most founders underprice is not that they don't know they should charge more. It's that they don't have a way to explain the price that feels honest. Like, you can feel when you're bullshitting. Your buyer can feel it too.

Generic cleaned-up draft

Many founders struggle with pricing. While experts often recommend higher price points, implementation remains challenging. The key is developing a clear value narrative that justifies your pricing to customers. By articulating the specific outcomes and ROI your product delivers, you can confidently raise prices while maintaining customer trust.

Voice-faithful rewrite

Everyone tells founders to raise their prices. That advice is correct and also useless.

The reason most founders underprice is not ignorance. It is that they do not have a way to explain the price that feels honest.

You can feel when you are bullshitting a number. The buyer can feel it too.

So the actual bottleneck is not a pricing model or a value metric. It is the language around the price. That is a writing problem, not a spreadsheet problem.

Questions skeptical buyers usually ask

How is this different from hiring a ghostwriter?

Most ghostwriters start from a brief or a topic list. This workflow starts from raw founder material — voice notes, transcripts, memos — and maps how the founder actually sounds before producing anything. The difference is measurable: a generic ghostwriter drifts toward interchangeable startup prose. This process preserves rhythm, vocabulary, and point of view.

Does this use AI? Will it sound like AI?

Language models are part of the editorial workflow, but they are not the product. The product is voice-faithful content. AI helps with speed and consistency; the voice brief and editorial judgment keep the output from sounding like generic AI text. The proof step exists precisely so you can judge this before committing.

What material do I need to send?

A small batch of anything that already sounds like the founder: a voice note, a podcast clip, a few internal messages, a transcript excerpt, or a piece of past writing you still like. Two to three items is enough for the audit. The more natural and unedited, the better.

What if the audit shows it does not work for us?

That is a valid outcome. The audit is designed to surface mismatches early. If the source material is too thin, or the founder's voice is not distinct enough to preserve meaningfully, the audit will say so rather than upsell into a sprint that will not land.

How long does the audit take?

Typically a few business days after receiving the source material. There is no long intake process or multi-week timeline. The goal is a fast proof point, not a research project.

Do I have to commit to a sprint or retainer afterward?

No. The audit is a standalone deliverable. If the proof is strong and the fit is clear, a sprint conversation makes sense. If not, you keep the audit and sample rewrite with no further obligation.
